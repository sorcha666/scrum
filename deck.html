Introduction

The Big Problem: Code Decay

Why planning everything upfront consistently fails.

Technical Debt When you quickly add new features to a project, the code gets messy. Over time, it becomes very hard to read, fix, or change. This is called technical debt.

The Old Ways

People used to plan the whole project perfectly before writing code. But it consistently failed because customers always change their minds along the way, leading to massive wasted effort.

The Agile Ways

Instead of planning everything, we work in small, careful steps. We build a little, test it continually, get feedback early, and adjust immediately. We expect and welcome things to change.

First, let's understand the problem. Imagine you're building an app. You plan everything perfectly at the start — then three months later, the customer says "actually, I want something different." Now all your planning is wasted. That's the old way. The new way — called Agile — says: don't plan everything. Build a little, show the customer, get feedback, and improve. Repeat. That's it.
1. XP

Extreme Programming (XP)

Building a Safety Net for Engineers

Extreme Programming is about taking the best coding habits and doing them constantly. It gives engineers a strict process so they never fear changing code.

The XP Cycle

Write a failing test.

Write simple code to pass.

Clean and Refactor.

1. FAIL
2. PASS
3. REFACTOR
The first method is called Extreme Programming, or XP. The idea is simple — take the best habits of good programmers and do them all the time, not just sometimes. The most important habit is this cycle: first you write a test that fails. Then you write just enough code to make it pass. Then you clean up your code. Then you do it again. This keeps your code clean and safe to change.
1. XP

XP Core Practices

Turning best practices up to maximum.

01

Test-Driven Development (TDD)

The test comes before the code. You define the target before you shoot. No ambiguity, no assumptions.

02

Pair Programming

Two engineers, one screen. One executes, one evaluates. What one misses, the other catches. The pace feels slower — the output is significantly better.

03

Continuous Integration

Code is merged and tested every single day. Problems surface immediately, not at the end of a two-week sprint when they've compounded into something much worse.

Engineering Perspective: XP turns good habits into mandatory, measurable rules that engineers follow on every line of code.

XP has three main practices. First, Test-Driven Development — you write the test before the code. This forces you to think clearly about what you want to build. Second, Pair Programming — two developers sit together at one computer. One writes, one watches and thinks. You catch more mistakes this way. Third, Continuous Integration — every developer merges their code every day and it's tested automatically. So problems are found immediately, not weeks later.
2. Lean

Lean Methodology

What is Lean Methodology?

The Lean methodology is a management approach that aims to:

Improve performance

Eliminate waste

Create more value for the customer

📌 Objective

Do more with fewer resources

Originally developed from Toyota's production system

The second method is Lean. Lean comes from Toyota — the car company. They had a simple question: how do we make more cars with less waste? The same idea works in software. Lean is about doing more with less — less time, less effort, less money — while still giving the customer exactly what they need.
2. Lean

The 5 Principles of Lean

The foundation of the Lean methodology.

1

Define customer value

2

Identify the value stream

3

Create continuous flow

4

Use a pull system

(produce on demand)

5

Continuous improvement

Example of continuous improvement: Kaizen — small, daily improvements made by every team member.

Lean is based on five principles. One — figure out what the customer actually values. Two — look at every step in your process and find which steps are useful and which are not. Three — make the work flow smoothly with no waiting. Four — only build things when someone asks for them, not before. Five — always look for small ways to improve every day. This last one is called Kaizen in Japanese.
2. Lean

The 7 Types of Waste

Over-production

Waiting time

Unnecessary transport

Over-processing

Excess inventory

Unnecessary motion

Defects

These seven wastes are collectively called Muda — and Lean's mission is to eliminate them all.

Lean says there are seven types of waste that slow teams down. Making too much. Waiting. Moving things around unnecessarily. Doing more work than needed. Having too much stuff sitting around unused. Unnecessary movement. And defects — bugs that need to be fixed. All of these waste time and money. Lean's job is to find them and remove them.
2. Lean

What Lean Helps You Achieve

Eliminate unnecessary activities

Improve quality

Reduce time

Optimize processes

Increase customer satisfaction

🎯 Goal

Continuous improvement and better performance for the entire team and customer.

When a team uses Lean properly, the results are clear. You remove activities that don't help. Quality goes up. Delivery time goes down. Your processes become smoother. And your customers are happier because they get what they want faster. The goal is always continuous improvement — a little better every single day.
3. Kanban

Kanban: A Visual Workflow Management System

Controlling flow. Eliminating bottlenecks. Delivering reliably.

Kanban is a method used to visualize work, control workflow, and improve efficiency across engineering teams.

Origin — Toyota Motor Corporation, 1940s

Developed as part of Toyota's production system. The word Kanban (看板) means "visual signal" or "card" in Japanese.

From Factory Floor → Software Teams

Initially used in manufacturing. Now widely adopted in software engineering and project management via tools like Jira and Trello.

Key Idea

"Work should flow through a system smoothly — without bottlenecks or overproduction."

The third method is Kanban. Kanban is a Japanese word that means "visual card" or "signal." It was invented at Toyota to track parts on the factory floor. Today, software teams use it to track tasks. The main idea is this: make your work visible. If you can see where everything is, you can spot problems before they become serious.
3. Kanban

Core Principles of Kanban

Treating work as a flowing, optimizable system.

01

Visualize Work

Tasks are represented as cards on a board — nothing is hidden.

02

Limit WIP

Engineers focus on a limited number of tasks at a time to maximize throughput.

03

Manage Flow

Monitor how work moves through the system and remove slowdowns.

04

Continuous Improvement

Teams regularly analyze and refine their workflow process.

Engineering Perspective: Kanban treats work as a flowing system — similar to process optimization in industrial engineering.

Kanban has four main principles. First, visualize your work — put every task on a board so everyone can see it. Second, limit how many tasks are in progress at once — doing too many things at the same time makes you slower, not faster. Third, manage the flow — watch how tasks move through your process and remove anything that slows them down. Fourth, always look for ways to improve your process.

Here is a real Kanban board. It has four columns: To Do, In Progress, Testing, and Done. Every task is a card. You move the card from left to right as the work progresses. The important thing here is the WIP limit — In Progress has a maximum of two tasks. If it's full, no new task can start until one is finished. This prevents the team from being overloaded. Watch what happens when we try to add a third task — the board blocks it. That's the whole point. Problems become impossible to ignore.

Kanban enables engineering teams to optimize workflow, reduce inefficiencies, and deliver work more reliably.

Kanban is used in software, systems, manufacturing, and research projects. The benefits are practical. You can see everything that's happening. Your team focuses on finishing tasks instead of starting new ones. Bottlenecks become visible immediately. And the whole team works at a steady, healthy pace without burning out.
🎓

Conclusion

Three methodologies. One goal — better engineering.

XP

Gives engineers a safety net to write tested, fearless code through TDD, pair programming, and CI.

Lean

Ruthlessly eliminates waste, stops multitasking, and focuses the team on delivering real value.

Kanban

Visualizes work flow, enforces WIP limits, and prevents team burnout through structured transparency.

Agile is not a process — it is a mindset. XP, Lean, and Kanban are the engineering tools that make it real.

So to summarize — XP helps your team write better, safer code. Lean helps your team stop wasting time and effort. Kanban helps your team stay organized and deliver work reliably. These three methods don't replace each other — many teams use all three together. The big idea behind all of them is the same: work in small steps, improve constantly, and always focus on what the customer actually needs. Thank you!
